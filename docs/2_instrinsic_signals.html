Neurovascular Coupling

Optical imaging methods do not measure neuronal activity directly. They measure vascular and metabolic changes that follow neural activity.

Neural signaling requires energy for:

  • action potentials
  • synaptic transmission
  • ion pumping
  • neurotransmitter recycling

All of these processes increase metabolic demand.

Neurovascular Unit

Neurons rely almost entirely on: glucose and oxygen. These are delivered by the vascular system. Neural activity therefore requires a local vascular response.

Neurovascular coupling refers to the set of mechanisms by which changes in neural activity lead to changes in:

  • cerebral blood flow (CBF)
  • blood volume
  • blood oxygenation

This coupling is local, regulated, and stimulus-dependent.

Neurovascular coupling is not controlled by neurons alone. It involves interactions between:

  • neurons
  • astrocytes
  • endothelial cells
  • smooth muscle cells

This is often referred to as the neuro-glio-vascular unit.

Neurovascular Mechanisms

There is not a single pathway linking neurons to vessels. Current models describe 3 main mechanisms:

  • direct neuronal signaling
  • astrocyte-mediated signaling
  • metabolic feedback signaling

These mechanisms act in parallel, not in isolation.

Mechanism 1: Direct neuronal signaling

Neurons can influence blood vessels directly.

This occurs through:

  • neurotransmitter release
  • neuromodulators
  • gaseous messengers (like nitric oxide, aka NO)

In this mechanism:

  • neural activity increases
  • neurons release vasoactive substances
  • nearby vessels dilate rapidly

This is often called a feedforward mechanism. It anticipates metabolic demand rather than reacting to it.

Properties of Direct Neuronal Signaling

Strengths

  • fast
  • spatially precise
  • tightly linked to synaptic activity

Limitations

  • not sufficient alone
  • does not encode metabolic state
  • varies across neuron types

This mechanism is responsible for the early vascular response.

Mechanism 2: Astrocyte-mediated signaling

Astrocytes:

  • sense synaptic activity
  • integrate signals over space and time
  • release vasoactive substances

They do not fire action potentials, but they actively regulate blood flow.

Typical sequence after synaptic activation:

  • glutamate activates astrocytic receptors
  • intracellular calcium rises
  • vasoactive signals are released (vasodilation)

This pathway is slower than direct neuronal signaling. Roles:

  • integrate and smooth fast neural fluctuations
  • contribute to spatial spread of vascular responses

This helps explain why vascular signals are broader than neural ones.

Mechanism 3: Metabolic feedback signaling

Neural activity alters local metabolism.

This leads to changes in:

  • oxygen levels
  • glucose consumption
  • carbon dioxide
  • pH

These metabolic changes influence vessel tone.

In this mechanism:

  • neural activity consumes oxygen
  • metabolic byproducts accumulate
  • vessels respond to restore balance

This is a feedback mechanism, not feedforward. It reflects actual energy use.

The three mechanisms work together

Neurovascular coupling emerges from:

  • fast neuronal feedforward signaling
  • intermediate astrocytic integration
  • slower metabolic feedback

Consequence for interpretation

Important implications:

  • signal amplitude ≠ firing rate
  • timing reflects vascular dynamics
  • spatial maps reflect integration

Despite its complexity:

  • coupling is reproducible
  • spatially organized
  • stimulus-dependent

This is why optical imaging works so well for mapping.

What does “intrinsic” mean?

Intrinsic signal optical imaging uses the brain’s own optical properties.

  • No dyes.
  • No fluorescent reporters.
  • No genetic indicators.

The signal emerges from:

  • hemoglobin absorption
  • blood volume changes
  • light scattering changes

Why the visual cortex?

The visual cortex is the ideal model system because:

  • it has precise topographic organization
  • responses are stimulus-locked

Retinotopy is a functional property of several visual areas:

  • neighboring points in visual space
  • are represented by neighboring points in cortex

Different visual areas have a complete (almost) representation of the visual field.

In ISI:

  • reflected light is recorded
  • The readout is a reflectance change over time.

Why reflectance changes

Hemoglobin absorbs light.

Oxygenated (OHb) and deoxygenated (RHb) hemoglobin:

  • have different absorption spectra
  • absorb differently at different wavelengths

Therefore: changes in oxygenation → changes in reflectance.

Wavelength matters

Different wavelengths emphasize different components.

Around 610-680 nm:

  • RHb absorbs strongly
  • active regions appear darker
  • signal correlates with increased HbR

Other wavelengths emphasize different dynamics.

Optical logic of ISI

Neural activation

  • increases oxygen consumption
  • transient increase in HbR
  • later increase in OHb

Optical consequence

  • reflectance decreases at HbR-sensitive wavelengths
  • spatially localized darkening
  • reproducible stimulus-locked maps

Early and late components

The intrinsic signal has components:

  • initial dip (early oxygen extraction)
  • hyperemic response (increased blood flow)
  • post-stimulus undershoot

These reflect the three mechanisms of neurovascular coupling.

Mapping retinotopy with ISI

Typical paradigm:

  • moving bar stimulus
  • drifting grating
  • phase-encoded visual stimulation

The cortical response:

  • shifts smoothly
  • mirrors stimulus movement
  • produces continuous retinotopic maps

The retinotopic organization is so strong that you can even draw on the visual cortex.

Beyond retinotopy

ISI has been used to map:

  • orientation columns (not in the mouse)
  • ocular dominance columns
  • somatosensory maps
  • auditory maps

It captures mesoscale cortical architecture.

ISI provides:

  • high spatial resolution (tens of micrometers)
  • large field of view
  • stable functional maps

This makes it ideal for cortical mapping in animals.

Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths

  • no dyes required
  • stable across sessions
  • excellent for mapping cortex
  • strong signal-to-noise ratio
  • ideal for primary sensory cortices studies

Weaknesses

  • signal is slow (seconds)
  • reflects vascular integration (indirect signal)
  • cannot resolve spikes or fast oscillations
  • limited to surface cortex
  • requires surgical preparation
  • influenced by anesthesia

What is fNIRS?

Functional Near Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a non-invasive method that measures changes in:

  • OHb
  • RHb

through near-infrared (NIr) light transmitted across the scalp.

Biological tissue has an optical window approximately between:

  • 700 nm and 900 nm

In this range:

  • light penetrates scalp and skull
  • hemoglobin absorbs selectively
  • water and lipids absorb less

This makes deep measurement possible.

From reflectance to absorption

In ISI:

  • light hits cortex
  • reflectance changes are measured

In fNIRS:

  • light enters scalp
  • travels through tissue
  • is partially absorbed
  • exits at a detector

We measure changes in light attenuation (Absorbance).

The Beer-Lambert principle

The core idea:

  • the amount of light absorbed depends on
  • the concentration of absorbing molecules
  • and the path length

In fNIRS, the absorbing molecules are:

  • OHb
  • RHb

We estimate concentration changes from light attenuation.

OHb and RHb absorb light differently (see absorption coefficients). Using at least 2 wavelengths:

  • allows separation of OHb and RHb signals
  • enables estimation of relative concentration changes

This is called the modified Beer–Lambert law.

fNIRS measures changes in:

  • OHb and RHb
  • Total hemoglobin (THb = OHb + RHb)

Typical activation pattern

During neural activation:

  • HbO increases
  • HbR decreases
  • blood volume increases

This pattern mirrors what we saw in ISI. The signal is vascular, not electrical.

Spatio-temporal Resolution

Spatial resolution:

  • centimeter-level spatial resolution
  • sensitivity to superficial cortex
  • no access deep brain structures.

Temporal resolution:

  • samples light at high frequency (tens of Hz)
  • but reflects slow vascular dynamics (seconds)

Temporal resolution is limited by physiology, not hardware.

Strengths of fNIRS

Advantages

  • non-invasive
  • silent (compared to fRMI)
  • portable
  • suitable for infants
  • usable in naturalistic tasks

Research relevance

  • social interaction studies (e.g. hyperscanning)
  • developmental research
  • clinical populations

fNIRS vs ISI

Intrinsic Signal Imaging

  • low invasive
  • reflectance
  • very high spatial resolution
  • animal models

fNIRS

  • non-invasive
  • absorption
  • low spatial resolution
  • human studies

Same physiology. Different optical constraints.

The fNIRS device: basic components

An fNIRS system consists of:

  • light sources
  • light detectors
  • optodes placed on the scalp
  • acquisition and processing unit

Sources:

  • emit NIr light
  • typically at 2 wavelengths
  • are placed on the scalp

Detectors:

  • measure light intensity exiting tissue
  • are placed a few centimeters away from sources
  • are sensitive to small changes in intensity

An optode is a general term for:

  • a source optode (emits light)
  • a detector optode (collects light)

Source-detector pairing

Optodes are arranged in structured arrays on the scalp.

A source and a detector form a channel. The distance between them is typically:

  • 2.5 to 3.5 cm in adults
  • shorter in infants

This distance determines sampling depth.

The light path forms a curved trajectory called Banana-shaped sensitivity profile. Maximum sensitivity occurs approximately:

  • halfway between source and detector
  • at a depth of about half the separation distance

Scalp blood flow contributes strongly. To control for this, systems include Short (separation) channels:

  • short channels (8–10 mm distance)
  • mainly sensitive to superficial layers of the tissue

These are used to regress out scalp effects.

Source-detector pairing

fNIRS setup requires attention to:

  • hair interference
  • optode contact
  • ambient light contamination

Signal quality depends strongly on preparation.
